Learning Guide

Why good AI videos need four settings

A video prompt alone is not enough. Product, character, storyboard and video settings work together to keep every shot accurate, consistent and useful.

The complete workflow

Build these four parts in order. Each one supplies information needed by the next stage.

1

Product Setting

What must stay accurate

Defines the product shape, packaging, colours, label zones and features that cannot change.

2

Character Setting

Who appears

Defines the same person’s face, age, hair, clothing, role and personality across every shot.

3

Storyboard Setting

What happens

Breaks the idea into scenes with a visual description, action, camera direction and voiceover.

4

Video Prompt

How it moves

Combines timing, camera movement, lighting, pacing, transitions and continuity constraints.

Why each part matters

1 · Product

Product Setting prevents visual mistakes

AI can change packaging, colours and small details between frames. Product Setting gives it a fixed reference.

  • Preserve real shape and proportions
  • Keep packaging and label zones stable
  • Avoid fake certifications or claims
  • Choose the correct product angle and lighting
2 · Character

Character Setting creates continuity

Without a character sheet, the person can look different in every scene.

  • Lock face shape, age and build
  • Keep hair and clothing consistent
  • Define role, expression and personality
  • Choose actions that match the campaign
3 · Storyboard Setting

Storyboard Setting turns an idea into scenes

It controls what the audience sees and hears instead of asking the model to invent the story.

  • Give every scene a basic description
  • Specify the action and focal point
  • Write voiceover scene by scene
  • Check timing before generating video
4 · Video

Video Prompt controls motion and finish

It translates the storyboard into technical direction for the video-generation model.

  • Set duration and aspect ratio
  • Define camera movement and transitions
  • Control lighting, mood and pacing
  • Prevent flicker, warping and identity changes

See the differences

Product choices affect clarity

A front view is best for packaging accuracy. Three-quarter views add depth. Top-down views help demonstrations, while macro views highlight texture and details.

Character choices protect identity

Face shape is one part of a repeatable character specification. Age, build, hair, clothing and expression must also remain consistent.

Storyboard Setting choices control the message

A product reveal, problem-to-solution story and tutorial each need different scenes. Transitions should support the intended pace, not distract from the product.

Storyboard Setting scene checklist

Every scene should answer these questions before video generation begins.

What does the audience see?
What does the character or product do?
What is the camera angle and movement?
What is the voiceover line?
How long does the scene last?
How does it transition to the next scene?
Which product details must remain accurate?
Which character details must remain unchanged?

Example four-scene structure

SCENE 1 · HOOK Description: A busy office worker notices an everyday hygiene problem at her desk. Voiceover: “Small routines can make a big difference.” SCENE 2 · PRODUCT INTRODUCTION Description: Clean three-quarter product reveal beside the character, with accurate packaging. Voiceover: “Meet a simple solution designed for everyday use.” SCENE 3 · DEMONSTRATION Description: Close-up of the character using the product correctly with natural hands. Voiceover: “Easy to use, wherever your day takes you.” SCENE 4 · CLOSING HERO Description: Stable front-facing product hero with the same character smiling softly in the background. Voiceover: “Make it part of your daily routine.”

Before generating the video

Upload the correct product reference
Approve one character setting
Confirm every scene and voiceover
Check total voiceover length against duration
Remove unsupported claims
Add negative prompts and continuity rules

See what a good prompt changes

A vague prompt leaves the model to guess. A production prompt fixes the product, character, composition, ratio and guardrails before generation.

Weak prompt

Too much is left to chance

“Make a nice picture of a person holding our product in a shop.”
  • No product shape, material or label rules
  • No character or hand guidance
  • No ratio, composition or negative prompt
Production prompt

Every important decision is controlled

“Create a 4:3 retail hero. Keep the white cylindrical pump bottle, teal label block and pump direction identical. Show a professional presenter with natural hands. Use clean navy-and-cream retail lighting. No cropped product, altered label, extra fingers, fake logo or unreadable text.”
  • Exact product continuity and character direction
  • Clear output ratio, composition and lighting
  • Negative prompts prevent common errors
